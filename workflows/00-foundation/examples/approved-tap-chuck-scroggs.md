## THE AVATAR — Meet Mike Holcomb

A composite of the real person we are speaking to. Every ad, email, SMS, landing page, and stage segment should be built as if Mike is the only person in the room.

**MIKE HOLCOMB, 47 — OWNER · HOLCOMB HVAC · MUSTANG, OK.** Took the business over from his dad in 2014. Twelve employees. Did $2.1M last year — and is more tired than he was at $800K. Married to Jenny, two kids in high school. First Baptist on Sundays. Drives a 2022 F-250. He doesn't need motivation. He needs structure. He doesn't want a coach. He wants a system. He's been burned before, and he's watching his neighbor's competitor pull ahead with "this AI stuff" he doesn't fully understand yet.

## 01 — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Our buyer is a **38–58 year old owner of an established Oklahoma service or retail business** doing **$500K–$5M in annual revenue**. They've been at it for 5–20 years, they've proven the model, and they've hit a structural ceiling. They have leads, customers, employees — but they are the bottleneck for everything, and the business cannot grow without consuming more of them personally.

They are **not a beginner**. They do not want a tactic. They are deeply skeptical of "agencies," "gurus," and anyone who has never made payroll. They want a peer who has walked their road, in plain English, with no hype, who can hand them a system that finally lets them lead the business instead of run it.

And right now, in 2026, there is one accelerant pulling them off the fence harder than anything else: **the AI conversation.** They feel it coming. They don't want to be the last guy on their street still doing it the 2018 way.

## 10 — THE AI ANGLE

Per the onboarding call, the event is being repositioned around an "AI Marketing Machine" angle. This is a strategic accelerant — but it has to be framed correctly for this audience.

**HOW MIKE HEARS IT — "I don't want to learn AI. I want to know what to do about it."** Mike is not a technology buyer. He is a **business owner watching his industry change.** Selling him "AI" sells him nothing. Selling him a **system that uses AI to fix his actual problem** — too few leads, too slow follow-up, too much owner-dependence — sells him everything. AI is the **vehicle**, not the destination. Lead with the outcome. Drop AI in as the mechanism. Never lead with the buzzword.

## 11 — MESSAGING — DO & DON'T

**✓ WHAT WINS**
- Plain-spoken English. Short sentences. Real words.
- Specific outcomes ("fill 20 calendar slots in 30 days") over vague promises.
- Local proof — Oklahoma names, Oklahoma businesses, Oklahoma towns.
- Faith-friendly without being preachy.
- Honor what he's already built. He's not a beginner.
- Frame the problem as structural, never personal failure.
- Owner-operator language ("payroll," "techs," "calendar," "books").
- Talk about family and time, not just money.
- Position AI as a tool he can master, not a threat that buries him.
- "Built by a fellow Oklahoma operator who walked this road."

**✕ WHAT LOSES**
- Hype. Shouting. "CRUSH IT!" "10X!" "EXPLODE!"
- Coastal / influencer aesthetic. Beach laptops. Lambos.
- Vague ("transform your business," "unlock your potential").
- Mocking his "small thinking" or "scarcity mindset."
- Selling AI as something that replaces him.
- Long, thought-leadery LinkedIn-poet copy.
- Anything that smells like Silicon Valley or Miami "agency life."
- Sales pressure he can detect (he uses it too).
- Generic stock photos of people in suits high-fiving.
- Promising "passive income" or "freedom in 30 days."
